Fostering Envy, Not Empowerment: The Dilemma of Female Incentives in STEM

By: Kylie Joy Morgan

In the realms of Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM), the call for gender diversity has been a persistent and crucial one. The need for equal representation of women in these fields is not just a matter of fairness but also of profound economic and social significance.
However, the strategies employed to incentivize women to enter and thrive in STEM careers have often been the subject of debate. From scholarships to mentorship programs, the incentives are varied, but are they truly empowering? Or are they inadvertently fostering a sense of envy and tokenism among females in STEM professions?

As a woman studying Computer Engineering, I have experienced and benefitted first hand with scholarships and job opportunities made available to myself in part because of my gender. Though this may appear to make a positive impact, I question, am I deserving of these opportunities? Or is there a more qualified individual that did not recieve such praise because they are not considered a minority.
In this article, I dissect the complex landscape of female incentives in STEM, aiming to understand their true impact on women's empowerment.

The Rise of Incentives for Women in STEM
Over the past few decades, there has been a noticeable positive shift in how society views the role of women in STEM. Initiatives aimed at encouraging girls to pursue these fields have multiplied, with scholarships, grants, and specialized programs becoming increasingly common. On the surface, these incentives appear to be positive steps towards addressing the gender gap in STEM. They seek to provide financial support, mentorship, and networking opportunities, all of which are crucial for anyone starting a career in a competitive field.
Scholarships specifically targeting women in STEM have been widely praised for their role in helping to overcome financial barriers. These scholarships often cover tuition fees, living expenses, and sometimes even travel costs for conferences or research opportunities. Organizations such as the 'National Girls Collaborative Project' and the 'Society of Women Engineers' have been instrumental in offering these financial incentives, recognizing the need to level the playing field for aspiring female scientists and engineers.
All of which was just stated is considered common knowledge. On the surface, there could'nt possibly be a downside to upbringing women in STEM fields. Well, not quite.

The Pitfalls of Tokenism and Envy

While the intentions behind these incentives are undoubtedly positive, there is a growing concern that they may have unintended consequences. One of the primary criticisms is the potential for tokenism, where women in STEM feel as though they are being used as symbols of diversity rather than valued for their skills and contributions. Research by Dr. Anna Fels, a psychiatrist and faculty member at Weill Cornell Medical College, explores the concept of "envy work" among women in male-dominated fields. She argues that women are often placed in positions where they are expected to embody certain qualities to fulfill diversity quotas or appear more inclusive. This can create a sense of resentment among female professionals, who may feel that they are being singled out or unfairly judged.
Moreover, the emphasis on gender-specific incentives can inadvertently reinforce stereotypes and biases. For example, the notion that women need extra support to succeed in STEM can perpetuate the belief that these fields are inherently more difficult for females. This can have a demoralizing effect, eroding confidence and perpetuating a cycle of self-doubt.

Moving Towards Empowerment-Centric Strategies

So, how can we navigate this delicate balance between incentivizing women in STEM without inadvertently fostering envy or tokenism? The key lies in shifting the focus from gender-specific incentives to empowerment-centric strategies.
One approach is to create inclusive environments that value diversity as a whole rather than singling out specific groups. This means promoting a culture of inclusivity where everyone, regardless of gender, feels supported and valued for their unique perspectives and contributions. Mentorship and networking programs should also be designed with empowerment in mind. Instead of pairing women with mentors solely based on gender, matching individuals based on shared interests, career goals, and values can lead to more meaningful and productive relationships.
Additionally, highlighting successful women in STEM not as exceptions but as the norm can help reshape perceptions. Celebrating achievements based on merit rather than gender can inspire future generations of female scientists and engineers.

Conclusion

The quest for gender diversity in STEM is an ongoing journey, one that requires thoughtful consideration of the incentives we provide to women entering these fields. While financial aid, mentorship programs, and networking opportunities are valuable tools, they must be implemented with caution to avoid inadvertently fostering envy or tokenism. By shifting towards empowerment-centric strategies, we can create environments where women feel supported, valued, and empowered to succeed based on their skills and merits. This approach not only benefits individual women but also contributes to a more diverse, innovative, and inclusive STEM community as a whole.
I know studying and working in engineering or STEM fields is hard. As a University of Waterloo student, I'm experiencing first-hand the inavoidable struggles of pursuing a challenging field. It is a fact this is a male-dominated field, and so promoting female networking opportunities to promote inclusivity is crucial to welcome more women in to this field. However, it is important to note this is challenging regardless of gender. And so, support and praise for success should be provided regardless of gender, and not to simply reach a diversity quota.
In the end, the goal is not just to get more women into STEM but to ensure that they thrive, grow, and become the leaders and innovators of tomorrow, free from the burdens of exclusivity, envy, and tokenism.
